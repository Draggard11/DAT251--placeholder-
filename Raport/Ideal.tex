\subsection{Design Thinking}
Overall, we followed many of the principles of design-thinking quite closely, especially in the starting phase of the project. We found the "\textit{introduction to design thinking}"\cite{DesignThinking} to give some good ideas on how to go about handling the non-trivial task to design software for other users than yourself, in a domain you have limited knowledge about. We really tried to understand the intent (the "why's") behind the responses we got in the interview. And when we were trying to condense the insights gathered from interviews, we found it useful with open-minded brainstorming. 

The review article "\textit{Design Thinking was supposed to fix the world...}"\cite{fix_the_world} brings up a lot of valid criticism towards Design Thinking, but a lot of the focus on the article is about issues in how Design Thinking has been marketed, and how it over-promises on fixing problems that are admittedly hard. Certain organizations (IDEO is brought up in the review) makes money on selling Design Thinking as a miracle cure, allowing designers to quickly become experts in any field, while the practices it highlights are really quite basic. Empathizing, Brainstorming and Prototyping are not novel ideas, and are used plentifully in other scenarios, and was not invented by these organizations who popularized and packaged them. 

We don't hold the design thinking process in such a negative view as can be interpreted from the article, but we believe this is due to how design thinking was presented to us. We were not "sold" design thinking as something that would solve all our problems, but rather an initial framework on how to start working in a software development process. The article does not mainly criticize the general practices, but rather misuse of these practices, and limitations in the approach when applied to large and complex systems. Of course, no one would disagree that empathizing or prototyping and feedback is good, but although trivial, for beginner developers such as us, a framework like this is a useful guiding structure. 

The criticism of the paper towards how design thinking has been marketed and misused, are not problems we have that much insight to, as we are still beginner developers and have not seen many such examples in practice. We used prototyping as a practical way to deliver something that could provide feedback and work as proof of concept for ourselves, our stakeholders, and as a deliverable for the project, not simply for the sake of prototyping. Although we mainly focused on a single prototype rather than continuously providing smaller iterations, contrasting the approach in the framework slightly.

Similarly for gathering stakeholder insights, we did not superficially empathize, nor did we claim to be experts afterwards. Rather we used the framework as pointers to how to go about interviewing, since none of us had particular experience with that from before. 

We also found that design thinking did not really help us with what we struggled with most, which was time constraints. Had we been more diligent with following the framework (such as making cardboard prototypes or pausing development to get feedback on individual features) or had been forced to repeat the process each sprint, it would likely worsen this problem as well. Iteration and validation requires time, and we can not necessarily assume we have time to spare.

\subsubsection*{Ideal Workflow}
For an ideal future workflow we would likely want to incorporate elements of design thinking as we generally view them as positive. We would however view them more in line in the same way we view pair-programming and test-driven development: As tools in a toolbox, rather than a rigid framework where every point must be followed exactly, as we think this would invite the issues highlighted in the review article.
